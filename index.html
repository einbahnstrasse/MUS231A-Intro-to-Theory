Descriptive vs. Prescriptive

  • The American musicologist Charles Seeger distinguished between prescriptive and descriptive types of music writing:
  • Prescriptive music writing: when notation serves as instructions for making particular musical sounds.
  • Descriptive music writing: when notation serves as record keeping or analysis of sounds produced.
  • Prescriptive: Dictates what “must” be done (e.g., Johann Joseph Fux’s rules for counterpoint).

Johann Joseph Fux, Gradus ad Parnassum (1725)

Descriptive vs. Prescriptive

  • Descriptive: Observes and explains what has emerged in practice, or what persists in a discourse (e.g., Gioseffo Zarlino describing how chords were already being tuned by composers of the day).
  • Over time, and depending on how the material is presented and internalized, descriptions can be mistaken for commandments.

Gioseffo Zarlino, Le istitutioni harmoniche (1558)

Emergence — Theory from Practice

Music theory today often arises from
descriptions of what people are doing...

It's a bit like deducing complex systems from data in the sciences.

Patterns emerge when many individuals make statistically similar choices over time.

So... What Is Music Theory?

Not commandments from above.

Not a fixed measure of beauty.

But a way of describing patterns that emerge,

of formalizing how we hear, group, and make sense of music.

And a reminder that our definitions will keep changing.
